# 01 — From Private Canon to Public Method

## Context
A private operational ecosystem had already accumulated constitutional documents, governance indexes, glossary concepts, FAQ material, contracts, seals, diaries and architectural notes.
The challenge was not creating a public narrative from zero. The challenge was translating a dense private canon into a publishable public layer without exposing sensitive internals or collapsing nuance.

## Problem
Direct publication of raw internal material would create multiple risks:
- leakage of operational detail;
- confusion between sovereign internal documents and public-facing explanations;
- public drift caused by premature publication of unstable or backstage material;
- loss of institutional intelligibility for external readers.

## Intervention
The response was to create a bounded public repository with a derived method:
1. identify which concepts were mature enough for public explanation;
2. redact and abstract internal material;
3. stabilize terminology against the canonical glossary and source-of-truth logic;
4. separate public explanation from internal sovereignty;
5. publish only what could be defended publicly without pretending completeness.

## Evidence
Evidence for this intervention consisted not in exposing raw internals, but in aligning the public layer with already existing constitutional, glossary and FAQ structures from the private canon.

## Limits
This case does not claim that public publication reproduces the full private architecture.
It demonstrates a method for translation, not equivalence between layers.

## Lessons
- transparency without curation is not maturity;
- a public repository gains trust when it names its boundary clearly;
- source-of-truth discipline matters even in outward-facing editorial work;
- the movement from private canon to public method can itself be a governance case study.
