# Debt Governance for Agentic Systems

Technical debt is already difficult in conventional software. In agentic systems, the problem becomes broader and more dangerous because the system does not only execute code. It also shapes interpretation, escalation, prioritization, response quality and operator trust.

That is why debt governance must be understood as more than technical cleanup.

## Debt is multidimensional
A serious agentic environment can accumulate at least five forms of debt:
- technical debt;
- documentary debt;
- evaluative debt;
- cognitive debt;
- governance debt.

Technical debt affects performance, reliability and maintainability. Documentary debt obscures what is canonical. Evaluative debt weakens the evidence about model behavior. Cognitive debt accumulates when reasoning pathways become opaque to operators. Governance debt appears when roles, approvals, checkpoints or accountability boundaries become blurry.

## Why debt governance matters
An agentic system may continue producing plausible output long after its integrity has begun to erode. That is what makes unmanaged debt especially deceptive. Surface utility can hide deep fragility.

A governed system therefore needs more than a bug backlog. It needs a disciplined way to know:
- what is pending;
- what is conflicting;
- what is degraded;
- what remains canonical;
- who owns remediation;
- which debt threatens the legitimacy of future actions.

## Governance changes the meaning of debt
In an agentic environment, debt is not only a cost to future engineering effort. It is also a risk to judgment, traceability and human control. A system with unresolved governance debt may still run, but it becomes progressively less trustworthy because its architecture stops making responsibility legible.

## Debt registry as operational instrument
A mature system treats debt registries as governance instruments, not as administrative clutter. Their role is to convert diffuse risk into explicit, prioritized and auditable queues. Without this, teams drift into ad hoc decision-making, and the system's true state becomes harder to name.

## Conclusion
Debt governance is not a side practice for mature phases. It is a constitutive requirement of serious agentic architecture. Systems that cannot name their debt cannot realistically claim that they are governed.
